WHERE IS OUR WATER GOING?


Drought isn’t a new phenomenon in the United States.

But recently, it’s been getting a lot of attention in the news


Drought is caused by climate variations, like changes in rainfall and evaporation rates...

But the drought we worry about is ultimately a human problem – drought can have huge social, economic, and environmental impacts because of how we manage water.


So how can we reduce our water consumption?


1) Taking shorter showers?

A 10 minute shower with an average U.S. showerhead would use 21 gallons of water.

If you halved that time, you’d be saving 10.5 gallons of water per day, or 73.5 gallons in a week.


2) Not watering your lawn?

If you ran a sprinkler from an average garden hose for 1 hour, you would use about 1020 gallons of water.

If you watered your lawn every other day, you’d be saving around 3060 gallons of water in a week.


3) Going vegan?

Around 92% of our daily water use is hidden in the food we consume, especially meats.


So if you normally ate a pound of beef per day (between lunch and dinner) and gave it up for a week, you would save 12915 gallons of water.

But between irrigation for crops and drinking water for livestock, the United States uses about 117 billion gallons of water per day.

That would take over 9 million people (about 9,060,000 or 3% of the US) all giving up a week of beef to counteract ONE day of industrial agriculture.

(Or, you could give up beef for 173,623 years.)


But if you don’t get your protein from meat, you have to replace it with other nutritional foods with their own water footprint…




But are these changes accessible?


This plan only works if you can afford the time and money to consciously change your diet and prepare meals with different foods.


Around 46,535,888 people in the US participate monthly in the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP).

...and around 20,400,000 people are living in deep poverty and most likely cannot provide the supplemental income for extra food.


So even if we could convince a portion of the population to cut certain water consumption-heavy foods out of their diet, it seems like we’re fighting a losing battle.


Even if we all were conscious of our lifestyle choices and had the ability to change them, this alone would not counteract the impact of drought.


So what else can you do?

How can we know where our water is going and effect change?
